About the technology

What AI actually does in air defense

Modern air and missile defense generates far more data than any human team can process in real time — radar returns, electro-optical feeds, signal intercepts, and satellite tracks, all arriving at once. AI is used to fuse these streams, flag threats as they emerge, recognize and classify targets, and surface the handful of decisions that actually need a person's attention.

In practice, that means faster threat detection, earlier trajectory warnings, and clearer situational awareness for the operators responsible for what happens next.

Recent academic research demonstrates that AI significantly improves threat detection, target classification, and real-time decision support by processing large volumes of sensor data more efficiently than traditional methods.

What it doesn't do: the system does not decide when force is used. It narrows a flood of data down to a recommendation — a human operator always reviews, validates, and authorizes before any action is taken.

Core applications

Where AI does the heaviest lifting

Four capability areas make up the backbone of any modern AI-assisted air defense system.

Threat Detection

Continuously scans radar, optical, and signal data to flag potential threats the moment they appear — including small or stealth objects a single sensor would miss.

Target Recognition

Classifies detected objects — aircraft, missile, drone, or friendly asset — using pattern recognition trained on historical sensor signatures.

Missile Interception

Predicts incoming trajectories and proposes the optimal interceptor and timing window, dynamically re-assigning assets as the situation evolves.

Sensor Fusion

Merges radar, electro-optical, and signal sources into a single unified track, resolving conflicts and gaps between individual sensors.

The hard part

Ethical challenges this technology raises

Deploying AI in a lethal-force context carries risks that any responsible program has to design around, not ignore.

RISK 01

Human control over lethal decisions

Without a hard-coded authorization step, AI recommendations could drift toward autonomous use of force — the single most important line to hold.

RISK 02

Bias in AI systems

Training data that under-represents certain scenarios can lead to skewed threat classification and unequal outcomes.

RISK 03

Errors & hallucinations

Models can produce confident but incorrect outputs, especially under incomplete or fast-changing battlefield conditions.

RISK 04

Cyberattacks & data manipulation

Adversaries who compromise sensor feeds or model inputs can manipulate what the system "sees," with cascading downstream effects.

RISK 05

Fully autonomous weapons

Removing humans from the loop entirely raises the risk of unintended escalation and unaccountable use of lethal force.

RISK 06

International regulation

Shared norms and enforceable agreements between nations are essential to prevent an unregulated military AI arms race.

Questions we get

Straight answers, no hedging.

Who makes the final call to engage a target?

A qualified human operator, every time. VIGIL surfaces a recommendation and the supporting evidence behind it — it does not have the authority to fire or engage on its own.

Can VIGIL be configured to act without human approval?

No. Removing the authorization step is not a configuration option — it's a hard architectural boundary, not a setting an administrator can turn off.

What happens when the system isn't confident about a track?

Low-confidence tracks are flagged as such rather than rounded up to a clean answer. Operators see the uncertainty directly instead of a false sense of precision.

Why is human oversight essential?

Because AI systems can be biased, produce confident-but-wrong outputs, or be manipulated through compromised data. A human in the loop catches what the model gets wrong before it becomes a real-world action.

Do operators need special training?

Yes — every deployment includes a certification program covering both the interface and how to evaluate an AI recommendation critically, not just accept it.
